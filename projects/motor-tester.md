title: Surgical Robot Motor Test Rig
---

In my return internship at Virtual Incision, a surgical robotics company, I designed and fabricated a test rig for the manufacturing line to quickly test a robotic arm's motor torque, cautery connection, and pneumatic seal.

The miniature surgical robot had motors that previously whose torque had only been assumed from line current values. I designed and built a fixture that the manufacturing and quality departments could load any robotic "forearm" into that used a torque sensor to read the throughput of motor output torque required to operate the tool end. I designed the fixture in SOLIDWORKS, created drawings to be submitted to a manufacturing company, and then assembled the fixture in house.

A lot of problems stemmed from just how small the motor was. To allow for accurate sensing, I had to locate the sensor axially to the motor extremely precisely. I did this with a keyway, which not only constrained fully one direction, but also allowed for an easy to use slide in-slide out set up. For the vertical direction, I knew the tolerance the company could achieve was not enough, so I created mounts consisting of two blocks that screwed together - this allowed me to insert shim stock to physically bring it up to the necessary dimension. For the rest of the misalignment, I added flexible shaft couplings. 

Not only this, but in talking to people from the manufacturing and quality side of things, I found that there was a desire to combine multiple tests onto one fixture to reduce fixture switching time. So on top of the torque sensor, I added two extra modules. For the first, I reoriented the motor in their driving-driven orientation so that electrical connectivity tests could be performed for cautery tools. And for the second, I designed an acrylic piece with an O ring, interface plate, and quick connect air fitting to create an add on to simultaneously test the robot's pneumatic seal during tool use.
